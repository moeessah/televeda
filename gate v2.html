Agnes Nez.

She is seventy-one years old. She lives on the Navajo Nation in northeastern Arizona. She served in the Army. She has a service-connected disability. She has not seen a VA provider in fourteen months. She is not in any social isolation dataset. She does not show up in the literature on Veterans' health, on Native American health, or on rural older adults, because she does not use the services that generate the data.

She is who this report is about.

Five findings

Every claim primary-sourced. Nothing here is secondhand.

Finding 01

The trajectory matters more than the snapshot

Four in ten Americans 45 and older are now lonely. The rate held stable for eight years, then accelerated sharply after 2018. No other major chronic health risk factor tracked at this population scale has moved this far in the wrong direction in a seven-year period. The snapshot shows a crisis. The trajectory shows a crisis that is not being managed.

AARP Research, December 2025
2

Finding 02

The health consequences are comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day

A 29% increased risk of coronary heart disease. A 32% increased risk of stroke. Significantly elevated dementia risk, now treated as a modifiable risk factor. Duration matters: the longer isolation goes unaddressed, the worse the physiological outcome. Health systems have built substantial infrastructure around tobacco cessation and largely not built equivalent infrastructure around social connection. The clinical evidence does not justify that gap.

Holt-Lunstad et al. 2010 / JAHA 2022 / J. Epidemiology and Global Health 2024 / Surgeon General's Advisory 2023
3

Finding 03

The populations carrying the most burden are the most consistently failed

A 2024 scoping review screened 1,500 publications on the health of American Indian and Alaska Native women Veterans. Twenty-eight met inclusion criteria. Most existing data came from women who already used VA services. The literature knows almost nothing about those who do not. Agnes Nez is one of those people. VA reports that about 1 in 3 women Veterans report military sexual trauma when screened in VA care. They are the fastest-growing demographic in the Veterans health system and among the hardest to reach through standard programming.

Women's Health Issues scoping review, 2024 / VA Women Veterans Health Care
4

Finding 04

Virtual group programming works, and for isolated populations it works better

The evidence for virtual group programming is not weaker than in-person. For geographically isolated populations, it is stronger, because it reaches people the in-person model cannot. The in-person model requires the participant to reach the program. The virtual model reaches the participant. For Agnes Nez, who cannot make a two-hour drive on unpaved roads that flood in spring and ice over in winter, the distinction is not technical. It is the difference between being served and not being served.

VHA CONNECTED Pilot, 2023 / Eur. J. Psychotraumatology, 2020
5

Finding 05

The gap is not evidence. The gap is implementation.

The evidence exists. The direction is clear. What is missing is funding directed at the right models, cultural adaptation that most existing programs have not achieved, and a willingness to design for the people who have been left outside the frame. Why programs fail these populations

A design failure, not a resource failure.

Most social isolation interventions were designed for a specific person: middle-class, English-speaking, technology-comfortable, within reasonable distance of a provider, and navigating systems built with their needs in mind. That person exists. They are also not who this report is about.

A wellness program translated into Diné Bizaad that retains the conceptual framework of individual psychological wellbeing is a translated program with untranslated assumptions. Navajo concepts of health are relational and collective. K'é, the kinship system through which a Diné person understands their place in the world, defines health as the maintenance of right relationship with family, clan, land, and community.

The Phoenix Indian School operated from 1891 to 1990. For nearly a century, federal policy actively dismantled k'é within the living memory of Agnes Nez and the elders these programs are trying to serve. That history shapes the conditions in which health programs now operate. Understanding it is not a preliminary step to effective design; it is the design work itself.

The failure to reach these populations is not a resource failure or a technology failure. It is a design failure rooted in assumptions so embedded they are rarely examined.
