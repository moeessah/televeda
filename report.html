Sourcing Statement

On Evidence and Honesty

This report cites primary sources throughout. Where the evidence is strong, we say so. Where it is emerging, we say that too. Where we draw on program evaluation data rather than independent research, we identify it as such. Key quantitative claims are paired with their source in the same section.

We distinguish throughout between peer-reviewed longitudinal evidence, government survey data, program evaluation findings, and participant self-report. These are different kinds of evidence. They warrant different weight. We have tried not to flatten that distinction in the service of a cleaner argument.

Where data carries significant methodological limitations, those limitations are stated in the body text.

Conflict of Interest

Disclosure

This report was produced by Televeda. Section Five presents program evaluation data from Televeda's own Arizona partnerships. That data is identified throughout as internal evaluation evidence, not independent research. All other findings draw on published primary sources, cited directly.

We have tried to present the evidence honestly, including where it is thin, where studies are small, and where long-term outcomes remain unknown.

Readers evaluating program partnerships with Televeda should weigh Section Five accordingly as the most specific data currently available for these populations under these conditions, presented with the limitations named.

Five Key Findings

01
The crisis is worsening, and the trend matters as much as the current rate. Adults aged 45 to 59 are now lonelier than those over 60, overturning one of the field's oldest assumptions. For many people who reach older age isolated, the slide began much earlier.
02
The health consequences are substantial. Social isolation is associated with a 29 percent increased risk of coronary heart disease and a 32 percent increased risk of stroke. The longer it goes unaddressed, the harder it becomes to reverse.
03
The populations carrying the greatest burden are the ones current programs most often miss: older adults in rural communities, Native American elders, Veterans, especially from post-9/11 service eras, and women Veterans who experienced military sexual trauma during service. Section Three explains why.
04
Group-based virtual programming produces measurable improvements in social isolation, perceived social support, anxiety, and depression among Veterans and older adults. For people who cannot make a multi-hour drive to a VA facility, virtual care is not a lesser substitute for in-person programming. It is often the only workable route.
05
The evidence gap is not the main problem. The real gap is implementation: funding directed to the right models, cultural adaptation that many programs still lack, and a willingness to design for people who have long been left outside the frame.

Section One

The People the Data Does Not See

Who is isolated, how the numbers are changing, and who bears the heaviest burden

A Distinction on Which This Report Depends

Social isolation is an objective condition: too little contact, too little relationship, too little support. Loneliness is something different. It is the felt experience of disconnection, and it can exist in a crowded room just as it can be absent in a sparse social network. The two are related, but they are not interchangeable, and they do not respond to the same interventions.

The three-item UCLA Loneliness Scale is a common screen for subjective loneliness; it is a flag for follow-up, not a diagnosis. PROMIS Social Isolation captures a different dimension: objective disconnection. This report uses both because the populations it describes often experience both at once, and conventional screening still misses much of that overlap.

Who Is Lonely and How the Numbers Are Moving

Adults aged 45 to 59 are now lonelier than adults over 60. The December 2025 AARP survey, which tracks the same population across 2010, 2018, and 2025, upends a long-standing assumption in this field: that social isolation is chiefly a problem of late life.

The break came after years of stability. The pandemic cut social ties at precisely the life stage when they are hardest to rebuild. At the same time, social media expanded the appearance of connection without restoring the institutions that once created it: congregations, clubs, neighborhood groups, and civic organizations. There is little reason to expect that loss to reverse on its own.

The Populations Bearing the Highest Burden

Rural older adults face barriers that urban program models were never designed to cross: distance, weak transportation networks, and chronic conditions that limit mobility before any supportive social infrastructure is in place. When the nearest senior center is a forty-minute drive and that drive becomes impossible, the program has to reach across the gap. The gap will not close itself.

Veterans experience a distinct version of the same problem. Social isolation often begins not in old age but in the years after separation from service. The 2023 CONNECTED study (Eliacin et al.) found 39% of surveyed Veterans reported significant social isolation, with especially high rates among Veterans under 55.

Women Veterans carry a distinct burden. Military sexual trauma is common among women Veterans; VA reports that about 1 in 3 women Veterans report MST when screened in VA care. PTSD is one of the most common mental health diagnoses related to MST, and women Veterans with MST histories have far higher PTSD rates than women Veterans without them (VA Women Veterans Health Care; Galovski et al., 2022). Women Veterans are the fastest-growing VA demographic and among the hardest to retain in standard social programming.

Native American communities face forms of structural disadvantage that the generic language of health disparity does not fully capture. On the Navajo Nation, some residents must travel 50 miles or more for basic services, and the reservation's broadband subscription rate was only 33% in the 2017–2021 ACS 5-year estimates.

Section Two

What Isolation Actually Costs

Health effects, mortality risk, and the financial case for acting earlier

Social isolation raises the risk of early death by about 30 percent, and that estimate has held up across large meta-analytic reviews. Wang and colleagues' 2023 meta-analysis of 90 prospective cohort studies involving 2.2 million people found social isolation associated with a 32 percent increased risk of all-cause mortality and a 34 percent increased risk of cardiovascular mortality.

The cardiovascular evidence is strong on its own. Valtorta and colleagues' 2016 systematic review in Heart, spanning 23 longitudinal datasets and more than 181,000 adults followed for up to 21 years, found poor social relationships associated with a 29 percent increased risk of coronary heart disease and a 32 percent increased risk of stroke. Those effect sizes are in range with risks clinicians already take seriously.

Duration compounds the problem. Chen et al.'s 2024 analysis found that persistent, unresolved isolation carried higher cardiovascular and mortality risk than temporary or episodic isolation. By the time a health system notices someone who has been isolated for years, the window for lower-cost prevention may already be closing.

Worked Example · Local Cost Modeling

A regional health system with 8,000 Medicare beneficiaries aged 65+, assuming a 25% social isolation prevalence consistent with the literature, faces roughly $3.2 million per year in excess Medicare spending attributable to isolation.

If a virtual group intervention reaching 500 of those beneficiaries costs $400,000 annually to operate and reduces isolation-related utilization by even 15%, the cost offset exceeds the program cost in the first year.

These figures are illustrative rather than predictive, but any health system can run the same calculation using its own population and utilization data.

The financial pressure of untreated isolation compounds the structural disadvantage that produced the isolation in the first place. In practice, this means health systems are systematically spending more on late-stage intervention in the same populations where early, lower-cost prevention is least available.

Section Three

A Design Failure, Not a Resource Failure

Why current programs miss rural older adults, Veterans, and Native American communities

Most social isolation interventions in the United States were designed around a particular user: middle-class, English-speaking, comfortable with technology, within reasonable reach of care, and moving through systems built with people like them in mind. That person is real. The people in this report are not that person.

"A program that was not built for you is not a program you failed to use. It is a program that failed to reach you."

Health Is Not an Individual Condition

Hózhó is the Diné framework for health, balance, and beauty: right relationship with self, family, community, land, and the living world. Kahn-John (Diné) and Koithan describe it as a wellness philosophy that guides thought, behavior, speech, and action. In that framework, broken relationship is itself a health problem. Restoring relationship is part of the intervention.

K'é, the Diné kinship system through which hózhó is lived and maintained, is the architecture of social life on the Navajo Nation. Clan relationships define obligation, belonging, and place. To be cut off from those relationships is to be unwell in a way English only partially captures.

Televeda's talking-circle model was built with Diné community leaders, traditional healers, and narrative therapists. When a participant introduces themselves in a Navajo Nation Talking Circle, the platform displays their name, tribe, and parents' clan. The k'é greeting that anchors Diné social life is preserved, and a virtual speaking token passes among participants as it would in person. The form carries the function.

The Design Incompatibility

A wellness program translated into Diné Bizaad but still built around individual psychological well-being carries its original assumptions with it. It treats the individual as the unit of intervention; hózhó and k'é treat the relationship as the unit. That mismatch cannot be fixed by translation alone. It requires a different design.

The Trust Deficit Has a Specific History and a Specific Author

The Phoenix Indian School operated in Arizona from 1891 to 1990. For nearly a century, the federal government separated Diné children from their families, prohibited them from speaking their language, and stripped away the clan names through which a Diné person understands where they belong. That outcome was not incidental to the school's mission. It was the mission.

The federal government dismantled k'é as explicit policy for a century. Many of the elders these programs are now trying to serve lived through the end of that era. Both VA and IHS are federal institutions. Programs that treat the resulting distrust as a messaging problem have misunderstood it from the start.

"We are very particular about not being parachute droppers, because historically, that has been the case with all nonprofits."

Mayank Mishra, Co-Founder, Televeda

Section Four

What the Evidence Actually Shows

What intervention research shows, how strong it is, and where the gaps remain

Across the intervention literature, peer connection is the active ingredient. Technology is a delivery mechanism, not the mechanism of change. A platform can distribute content, but if it does not build durable community, it is not doing the same work.

Strongest Evidence
In-Person Group Programming
Structured, recurring group programming reduces both subjective loneliness and objective isolation over time. Effects can persist at six- and twelve-month follow-up when the program itself lasts long enough. Its main limitation is reach: most of the evidence comes from people who can attend in person. Rural, homebound, and tribal adults are largely missing from that evidence base.
Growing Evidence · Most Relevant Here
Group-Based Virtual Programming
In the CONNECTED study (Eliacin et al., 2023), a peer-facilitated virtual telehealth group intervention was piloted with 19 Veterans across two cohorts. The study found lower social isolation, greater perceived social support, and improvements in anxiety and depression. Just as important, telehealth removed access barriers that would have prevented participation in the first place.
Moderate, Short-Term Effects
One-to-One Contact
Telephone and video-based peer contact can reduce loneliness in the short term for homebound older adults (Masi et al., 2011). Effects are generally smaller than those of group-based interventions and less likely to last once the program ends. A one-to-one relationship can start connection; it does not automatically create community.
Evidence Gap
AI/AN-Specific Programs
Good et al.'s 2024 scoping review in Women's Health Issues, which screened 1,508 publications and retained 28, found that most existing evidence came from women already engaged with VA services. That is part of why Televeda's Navajo Nation program data matters: there is very little implementation evidence for this population.

Format Matters: Synchronous vs. Asynchronous

Evidence on remote connection remains limited and highly dependent on design. A 2020 rapid review of video-call interventions in older adults found a small and uncertain evidence base. The National Academies' 2020 report treats technology primarily as infrastructure for connection, not as an intervention in its own right. For health systems and tribal communities making infrastructure decisions, the more relevant standard is support for real-time video participation, not message delivery alone.

"The gap is not evidence. The evidence exists. The gap is implementation, will, and money directed at the right models."

Section Five

Five Years, One State

What implementation evidence looks like in Televeda's Arizona partnerships

The data from Televeda's Arizona partnerships is presented as program-evaluation evidence, not controlled-trial findings. It is the most specific evidence available for these populations under these conditions.

VA Mission Daybreak — 1st Place Fast Company — 2023 Honoree AZ Dept. Health — 5-Year Evaluation

In February 2023, the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs named Televeda a first-place winner in Mission Daybreak, a $20 million challenge within the VA's long-term suicide-prevention strategy, for Project Hózhó. Fast Company later named the work a 2023 World Changing Ideas honoree. Arizona health-improvement materials have also highlighted Televeda in connection with telehealth access in underserved communities.

The Model Operates on Three Principles

1
Sustained Peer Community, Not Content Delivery
The aim is not content delivery. It is to build durable peer relationships. That requires consistent groups, consistent facilitators, and enough time for trust to form. A group that meets briefly and disappears has not delivered the intervention.
2
Cultural Adaptation as a Design Requirement, Not a Feature
Talking circles are built around Diné kinship practices because Diné community leaders, healers, and narrative therapists helped design the model from the start. This was not a late-stage adaptation. It was core design work.
3
Reach as the Foundational Design Intent
Through the Arizona Department of Veterans' Services' Tribal Connectivity Project, "Roger That," Televeda and ADVS are deploying Starlink internet kits across Arizona's 22 federally recognized tribes. Four tribes have already received kits: the Gila River Indian Community, the Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community, the San Carlos Apache Tribe, and the Navajo Nation.

"The Arizona Department of Veterans' Services is thrilled to bring internet access to our rural and tribal communities. Partnering with community organizations to deliver this vital resource aligns perfectly with our mission."

Dylan Dalzotto · Assistant Deputy Director, Public & Intergovernmental Affairs, ADVS

1
Screen for Social Isolation as a Standard of Care by End of 2026
The three-item UCLA Loneliness Scale and PROMIS Social Isolation are both validated for clinical use and take less than five minutes to administer. CMS Z-codes now provide a billing mechanism for documenting social isolation in the clinical encounter. Screening is most effective when paired with a clear referral pathway. Before launching any screening initiative, map the referral options in your catchment area, including virtual programs for rural and homebound patients.
2
Measure Outcomes That Matter, and Measure Them Consistently
Attendance and satisfaction are easy to collect and poor proxies for health impact. Programs contracted by health systems should report changes in validated loneliness and isolation scores at baseline, three months, and twelve months at minimum. The $1,608 annual per-person cost differential documented by Flowers et al. (2017) provides a practical starting point for cost-benefit modeling.
3
Require Documented Cultural Adaptation Before Contracting
A social isolation program designed for urban older adults and extended to Native American or rural Veteran communities without redesign is not truly serving those communities. Health systems procuring social programming for these populations should require documented evidence of community co-design: named community partners, described adaptation processes, and demonstrated facilitator cultural competency built through lived relationship rather than pre-deployment training. When that documentation is missing, it should be treated as a meaningful signal.
4
Require IHS and VA Coordination for Any Program Serving Native American Veterans
A Native American Veteran may be eligible for services from both systems, but neither system was designed to coordinate with the other. Any program serving this population should include an explicit coordination mechanism between IHS and VA staff as a design requirement, not an aspiration. Tribal Veterans Representative programs have already shown that this gap can be bridged, as VA MIRECC VISN 19 documented in 2024.
5
Fund the Infrastructure Before the Program
Device access and reliable internet that can support real-time video participation are prerequisites, not amenities. 42% of Veterans residing in rural or highly rural areas enrolled in VA healthcare do not have home internet capable of supporting VA telehealth services (VA Office of Health Equity). Programs launched into populations without that infrastructure predictably produce low enrollment and early dropout, then misread those failures as participant disengagement.
6
Map the Federal Funding Streams Before Writing the Next Grant
The following mechanisms are already in circulation and have been used to fund social-isolation and community-connection programs for older adults, Veterans, and tribal communities:
Older Americans Act (ACL)
Funds work through Area Agencies on Aging and tribal organizations
HRSA Federal Office of Rural Health Policy
Community-based health initiatives for underserved rural and tribal populations
VA Mission Daybreak
Funds suicide prevention innovations specifically targeting AI/AN Veterans
VA Community Care Program
Funds supplemental services for Veterans who cannot access VA care due to distance
ACL ARPA Social Isolation Investments
A newer and underused stream for organizations that already have an evidence base. It will not solve the funding problem, but it is more accessible than many program planners assume. Mapping the gap between available funding and actual need is more productive than waiting for the landscape to improve on its own.

Methodology

This report synthesizes peer-reviewed literature, federal survey data, and government reports published between January 2020 and March 2026. Sources were identified through PubMed, PsycINFO, and Google Scholar searches, supplemented by materials from VA, IHS, ACL, HRSA, GAO, and CMS. We prioritized systematic reviews and meta-analyses, then large longitudinal studies, followed by smaller trials and program evaluations. Material methodological limitations are noted where relevant.
