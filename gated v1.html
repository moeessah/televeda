What the evidence actually says, at what level of rigour, for which populations, and under which conditions.

Finding one

The crisis is accelerating. The trajectory matters more than the snapshot.

Four in ten Americans aged 45 and older are now lonely, a figure that rose five percentage points between 2018 and 2025, per AARP's December 2025 survey tracking the same population across fifteen years. Adults aged 45 to 59 are now lonelier than those over 60. The snapshot shows a crisis. The trajectory shows one that is not being managed.

AARP Research, December 2025 ↗

Finding two

The mortality risk is comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Health systems have not responded proportionately.

A 2015 meta-analysis drawing on data from 3.4 million participants found that loneliness and social isolation are associated with a 26% and 29% increased risk of premature mortality respectively, a magnitude comparable to smoking up to 15 cigarettes a day and one that exceeds the risk associated with obesity. Health systems have built substantial infrastructure around tobacco cessation. The evidence does not justify failing to do the same for social connection.

Holt-Lunstad et al., Perspectives on Psychological Science, 2015 ↗

Finding three

The highest-burden populations are the ones current programs most consistently fail to reach.

This is not coincidence. Older adults in rural and underserved communities, Native American elders, Veterans, and women Veterans outside metropolitan areas were not the populations existing intervention models were designed for. They remain outside the frame because the reach was never attempted in the right way. The design failure is the subject of Section 4.

Finding four

Virtual group programming is not a substitute for in-person care. For isolated populations, it is the only viable route.

A 2023 Veterans Health Administration pilot of the CONNECTED intervention found statistically significant improvements in social isolation scores, social support, anxiety, and depression at three months. For the woman two hours from a VA clinic on unpaved roads, the virtual model does not replace in-person care. It makes care possible where it was not possible before. This is pilot-level evidence, presented as such.

Foote and Tsai, VHA Pilot Study, 2023 ↗

Finding five

The gap is not evidence. The evidence exists. The gap is implementation.

Funding directed at the right models. Cultural adaptation that most existing programs have not achieved. A willingness to design for the populations that standard care was not designed for in the first place. Why programs fail these populations

A design failure. Not a resource failure.

Most social isolation interventions were designed for a specific person: middle-class, English-speaking, technology-comfortable, within reasonable reach of a provider, and navigating systems built, however imperfectly, with their needs in mind. That person exists. They are also not who this report is about.

A wellness program translated into Diné Bizaad that retains the conceptual framework of individual psychological wellbeing is a translated program with untranslated assumptions. Navajo concepts of health are relational and collective. K'é, the kinship system through which a Diné person understands their place in the world, defines health as the maintenance of right relationship with family, clan, land, and community. A program built around the individual reconnecting does not understand what "community" means in this context.

The Phoenix Indian School operated from 1891 to 1990. The federal government dismantled k'é as explicit policy for one hundred years, within the living memory of the elders these programs are trying to serve. Programs that treat that distrust as a messaging problem to be solved with better branding have misread what the distrust is and where it came from.
