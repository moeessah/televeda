Scale and cost

What isolation actually costs, and why current systems treat it as someone else's problem.

The mortality risk of chronic social isolation is comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day, a magnitude exceeding that of obesity, and one that health systems have not responded to proportionately. Tobacco has infrastructure. Social connection does not.

Social isolation among midlife and older adults is associated with an estimated $6.7 billion in additional Medicare spending annually. That figure was calculated from 2017 and 2018 data. Loneliness prevalence has since risen five percentage points. The current cost is almost certainly higher. No updated analysis at this scale had been published at the time of writing.

The cost burden falls hardest on the systems least equipped to absorb it. Tribal health programs operating under the Indian Health Service serve populations with elevated isolation risk while operating under chronic funding constraints. The VA's rural health program faces the same compression. The financial pressure of unaddressed isolation compounds the structural disadvantage that produced the isolation in the first place.

The five findings

What the evidence actually says: at what level of rigour, for which populations, under which conditions.

01

Finding one

The crisis is accelerating. The trajectory matters more than the snapshot.

Four in ten Americans aged 45 and older are now lonely, a figure that rose five percentage points between 2018 and 2025, according to AARP's December 2025 survey tracking the same population at three points across fifteen years. The rate held stable for eight years then broke. The snapshot shows a crisis. The trajectory shows a crisis that current systems are not managing.

AARP Research, December 2025 ?
02

Finding two

The health consequences are comparable to tobacco. The response infrastructure is not.

A 2015 meta-analysis drawing on data from 3.4 million participants found that loneliness and social isolation are associated with a 26% and 29% increased risk of premature mortality respectively, a magnitude the researchers compared to smoking up to fifteen cigarettes a day, and one that exceeds the risk associated with obesity. A separate 2016 systematic review found associated risks of 29% for coronary heart disease and 32% for stroke. Health systems have built substantial infrastructure around tobacco cessation. The evidence does not justify failing to do the same for social connection.

Holt-Lunstad, Smith, Baker, Harris, Stephenson. Perspectives on Psychological Science, 2015 ?
03

Finding three

The highest-burden populations are the ones current programs most consistently fail to reach.

This is not coincidence. The populations bearing the highest burden (older adults in rural and underserved communities, Native American elders and Veterans, women Veterans outside metropolitan areas) are the ones existing intervention models were not designed for. They remain outside the frame because the reach was never attempted in the right way. The design failure is the subject of Section four.

04

Finding four

Virtual group programming reaches people the in-person model structurally cannot.

Pilot and observational studies of group-based virtual programming, including a 2023 VHA feasibility study of the CONNECTED intervention, show consistent positive changes in social isolation scores, social support, anxiety, and depression at three months. For geographically isolated populations, the virtual model is not a substitute for in-person programming. It reaches people the in-person model cannot, because it does not require the participant to reach the program. This is presented as pilot and observational evidence. It is not presented as RCT-grade evidence. The field needs that trial. Televeda intends to contribute to producing it.

Foote and Tsai, 2023 ?
05

Finding five

The gap is not evidence. The evidence exists. The gap is implementation.

The gap is funding directed at the right models, cultural adaptation that most existing programs have not achieved, and a willingness to design for the people who have been left outside the frame. Section six contains five specific recommendations with timelines, including the federal funding streams most health system leaders have not yet mapped against their planned programs.

DESIGN

Why programs fail these populations

A design failure, not a resource failure.

Most social isolation interventions were designed for a specific person: middle-class, English-speaking, technology-comfortable, within reasonable distance of a provider, and navigating systems built, however imperfectly, with their needs in mind. That person exists. They are also not who this report is about.

A wellness program translated into Diné Bizaad that retains the conceptual framework of individual psychological wellbeing is a translated program with untranslated assumptions. Navajo concepts of health are relational and collective. K'é, the kinship system through which a Diné person understands their place in the world, defines health as the maintenance of right relationship with family, clan, land, and community. A program built around the individual reconnecting does not understand what "community" means in this context.

The Phoenix Indian School operated from 1891 to 1990. For nearly a century, federal policy actively dismantled k'é within the living memory of Agnes Nez and the elders these programs are trying to serve. That history shapes the conditions in which health programs now operate. Understanding it is not a preliminary step to effective design; it is the design work itself.

"The failure to reach these populations is not a resource failure or a technology failure. It is a design failure rooted in assumptions so embedded they are rarely examined."
